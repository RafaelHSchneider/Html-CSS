Romulus and Remus

The founding of Rome is steeped in myth and legend, with the story of Romulus and Remus serving as its most famous origin tale. According to Roman mythology, the twins were born to Rhea Silvia, a Vestal Virgin and daughter of Numitor, the deposed king of Alba Longa. Their father was believed to be Mars, the god of war. Fearing a threat to his rule, Amulius, Numitor's brother, ordered the infants to be abandoned on the banks of the Tiber River. However, they were saved by a she-wolf who nursed them and a woodpecker who fed them until a shepherd named Faustulus found and raised them as his own.

As adults, Romulus and Remus learned of their royal heritage and decided to establish a city near the spot where they had been saved. However, a dispute arose over who would rule and where the city would be built. Romulus favored the Palatine Hill, while Remus preferred the Aventine Hill. The conflict escalated, and in a fit of rage, Romulus killed Remus. He then founded the city of Rome on the Palatine Hill in 753 BCE, becoming its first king. The city was named after him, and its early history was marked by efforts to attract settlers, including the infamous abduction of the Sabine women to ensure its population growth. This legendary tale, while not historically verifiable, became a cornerstone of Roman identity and cultural pride.
